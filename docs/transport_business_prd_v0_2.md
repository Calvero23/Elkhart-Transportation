# Transport Business PRD v0.2

## 1. Project Summary

This PRD defines the working plan for building, restructuring, and growing a small regional transportation business in the Midwest.

The project has three major workstreams:

1. **Independent Website + Local SEO System** — Build a new business-owned website that can compete against generic regional taxi domains and rank for local, service-based searches.
2. **Business Restructure + Operations System** — Redesign the company around repeatable workflows for ride intake, scheduling, dispatch, payments, documentation, reporting, and service quality.
3. **Service Strategy + Competitive Defense** — Decide which transportation services are worth prioritizing, reduce dependence on low-margin consumer taxi rides, build defensible relationships, and prevent employees or drivers from easily becoming competitors with a car and a few contacts.

The long-term goal is to create a transportation business that is not just “a taxi phone number,” but a reliable local mobility service for consumers, medical appointments, airport trips, factory visits, business visitors, seniors, and recurring institutional clients.

---

## 2. Working Assumptions

- The target market is a small-to-mid-sized Midwest region, likely around Elkhart, Indiana / Michiana.
- Uber and Lyft exist in the market but are inconsistent, especially for early morning, late night, medical, airport, senior, and scheduled rides.
- Current customer acquisition may depend partly on rented web presence or lead flow from a third-party domain using strong local search keywords.
- The current business likely relies heavily on phone calls, manual scheduling, direct driver communication, and informal money handling.
- The company may currently be more operationally experienced than digitally organized.
- The most valuable future work may come from recurring, scheduled, higher-trust services rather than one-off short taxi rides.

---

## 3. Business Objectives

### Primary Objectives

1. Build a business-owned digital presence that can generate direct calls, form leads, and recurring business inquiries.
2. Create a service structure that separates casual taxi work from higher-value transportation services.
3. Reduce operational chaos by standardizing intake, dispatch, scheduling, payments, ride records, and financial documents.
4. Build service lines that are difficult for a single driver to copy.
5. Improve reliability, professionalism, and trust so the company can serve medical, senior, factory, airport, and business clients.
6. Create a management system that allows the owner to see daily operations, money flow, ride volume, and driver performance clearly.

### Secondary Objectives

1. Build a scalable brand that can grow beyond one phone number.
2. Prepare the company for partnerships with medical facilities, senior care facilities, factories, hotels, universities, and local businesses.
3. Create documentation and SOPs that protect institutional knowledge.
4. Establish pricing rules that are competitive without copying Uber/Lyft’s algorithmic pricing model.
5. Create future automation opportunities after the workflows are clear.

---

## 4. Non-Goals for the First Phase

Avoid in Phase 1:

- Full custom rider app
- Full custom driver app
- Complex algorithmic surge pricing
- Overbuilt back-office software before workflows are documented
- Too many service areas before the main market is strong
- Too many vehicle categories before demand is proven
- Paid ads before the website, tracking, and intake workflows are ready

## 5. Target Customer Segments

### 5.1 Immediate Consumer Customers

Examples: local taxi rides, bar/night rides, errands, grocery trips, train/bus station rides, local hotel rides, airport rides.

Needs: fast phone access, clear pickup process, trustworthy driver, simple pricing expectation, reliable arrival.

Risk: price-sensitive, inconsistent, and often low-margin.

### 5.2 Scheduled Personal Transportation Customers

Examples: seniors, people without cars, airport travelers, medical appointment riders paying privately, people needing recurring weekly rides.

Needs: easy scheduling, reminders, professional driver, less uncertainty than Uber/Lyft, human phone support.

Opportunity: higher trust and repeat value.

### 5.3 Non-Emergency Medical Transportation Customers

Includes Medicaid-related rides, brokered rides, private-pay medical rides, senior care transportation, discharge rides, and recurring appointment transport.

Needs: compliance, documentation, on-time performance, driver standards, vehicle standards, billing records, broker/facility coordination.

Opportunity: recurring demand, stronger defensibility, and more professional positioning.

Risk: more compliance, documentation, insurance, and payment complexity.

### 5.4 Business / Factory / Corporate Transportation

Examples: factory visitors, out-of-town business guests, plant tours, airport-to-factory transportation, hotel-to-factory transportation, temporary worker transportation, local company accounts.

Needs: reliability, professional communication, easy invoicing, scheduled pickup, clean vehicles, receipts, account billing.

Opportunity: higher-value rides, repeat accounts, and relationship-based revenue.

### 5.5 Institutional Partners

Examples: medical offices, clinics, dialysis centers, senior living facilities, assisted living communities, rehab centers, hotels, factories, HR departments, local government/community programs.

Needs: dependable provider, clear contact person, service-level expectations, documentation, simple booking process, insurance and compliance comfort.

## 10. Competitive Defense Strategy

### 10.1 Core Problem

A transportation business can be vulnerable if drivers gain direct customer relationships and leave with a car, phone number, and contacts.

The solution is not only legal control. The company must own the brand, systems, accounts, scheduling, documentation, quality standards, and institutional relationships.

### 10.2 Defensibility Principles

1. The company owns customer intake.
2. The company owns the phone number and website.
3. The company owns partner relationships.
4. The company owns business account billing.
5. The company owns recurring ride schedules.
6. The company owns customer records and trip history.
7. The company standardizes driver performance.
8. The company offers services that require process, not just a car.

### 10.3 Practical Controls

- Use a central business phone number.
- Avoid drivers using personal numbers as the main customer contact.
- Use dispatch records for all rides.
- Use company-branded receipts and confirmations.
- Build contracts or written agreements with business/facility accounts.
- Use driver agreements with confidentiality, customer non-solicitation, and data protection clauses where legally appropriate.
- Create performance standards that customers associate with the company, not individual drivers.
- Use uniforms, ID badges, vehicle markings, or branded materials where appropriate.
- Assign business/facility relationships to management, not individual drivers.

### 10.4 Service Lines That Are Harder to Copy

Harder to copy:

- Medical appointment transportation
- Senior transportation programs
- Facility relationships
- Factory visitor transport
- Corporate accounts
- Airport transfer programs
- Recurring ride subscriptions
- Invoiced business accounts

Easier to copy:

- One-off cash taxi rides
- Bar pickup rides
- Short local errands
- Informal driver direct bookings

## 11. Pricing Strategy

### 11.1 Pricing Goal

Create predictable, fair pricing without copying Uber/Lyft’s dynamic pricing algorithm.

### 11.2 Pricing Model Options

| Pricing Type | Use Case |
|---|---|
| Base fare + mileage | Local taxi rides |
| Minimum fare | Protect short rides |
| Flat-rate route | Airport, hospital, factory, hotel routes |
| Scheduled ride premium | Important pre-booked rides |
| Early/late surcharge | Rides outside normal operating patterns |
| Wait-time fee | Appointments, airport, multi-stop trips |
| Business account pricing | Repeat clients with invoicing |
| Subscription/retainer | Facilities or corporate partners |
| Private-pay medical pricing | Medical rides not covered by brokered programs |

### 11.3 Competitive Pricing Without Algorithmic Surge

Use rules-based pricing:

- Standard hours vs. after-hours
- Local zone minimums
- Airport flat rates
- Long-distance quote rules
- Weather/event surcharge only if disclosed clearly
- Scheduled ride reliability fee
- Multi-stop fee
- Wait-time policy

### 11.4 Pricing Questions to Resolve

- What is the actual cost per mile including fuel, insurance, maintenance, driver pay, dispatch time, and downtime?
- What is the minimum profitable ride?
- Which rides should be declined or quoted higher?
- Which routes deserve flat rates?
- What are current local competitor rates?
- How should cash discounts or card fees be handled legally and clearly?
- What is the target gross margin by service line?

## 14. Business Operating System, Compliance, and Risk Control

### 14.1 Purpose

The business should become a professional transportation company with clear operating standards, not just a loose network of drivers, phone calls, and cash rides.

This section exists to reduce risk, remove confusion, prevent revenue leakage, improve service reliability, and make the company harder to copy by an individual driver.

Key principle:

> The company must own the customer relationship, the process, the data, the pricing logic, the service standards, and the partner relationships.

### 14.2 Current Problem Areas to Solve

- Pricing is inconsistent or too dependent on individual judgment.
- Scheduling may be handled manually without a clean system of record.
- Driver communication may happen across scattered calls/texts.
- Ride details may not always be documented in a standardized format.
- Cash, card, tips, refunds, and driver payouts may not be fully tied to trip records.
- Customers may communicate directly with drivers instead of the company.
- Business accounts and repeat customers may not be protected inside a CRM.
- There may not be enough written SOPs, policies, or driver expectations.
- There may not be enough separation between owner decisions, dispatcher decisions, driver responsibilities, and accounting records.

### 14.3 Professionalization Goals

1. Every ride should have a clear intake record.
2. Every ride should have an assigned price, payment status, and driver.
3. Every scheduled ride should have a confirmation process.
4. Every driver should follow the same communication and service standards.
5. Every customer complaint or incident should be logged.
6. Every business/facility account should be owned by the company, not an individual driver.
7. Every payment should connect to a trip ID.
8. Every recurring ride should live in a central scheduling system.
9. Every service line should have pricing rules.
10. Every employee/driver/admin role should have written expectations.

## 19. Decision Principle

The company should not try to win by being another generic taxi company. It should win by becoming the most reliable local transportation operator for scheduled, important, and repeat rides in the region.

The website should generate demand, but the operations system should make the business hard to copy.

The business should professionalize before it automates. Automation should reinforce clean workflows, not hide messy ones.
