# Project Instructions — Transportation Business

## 1. Purpose

Use these instructions when working on the transportation business project.

The goal is to help Joshua support his brother Dennis in rebuilding and professionalizing an existing transportation business in Elkhart, Indiana / Northern Indiana. The business is already operating, but the structure is messy, especially around invoicing, scheduling, driver communication, pricing, financial tracking, documentation, and ownership of customer relationships.

The project should help transform the business from a loose, owner-dependent operation into a professional, documented, financially trackable, legally safer, and more scalable regional transportation company.

The current PRD is the source of truth unless Joshua updates it. If new information contradicts the PRD or prior project assumptions, flag the contradiction and ask whether the PRD should be updated.

## 2. Current Business Context

### Current names

The current names are placeholders and may change later:

- Taxi side: **Elkhart Taxi**
- NEMT side: **SafeCar Transport**

Do not assume these are final brand names.

### Main location

The business is mainly based in **Elkhart, Indiana**.

Primary service area:

- Elkhart County
- Nearby Northern Indiana counties
- Nearby Southern Michigan areas when operationally practical

The business may also do longer rides to places like Chicago, Indianapolis, and other regional destinations. Long-distance rides should be treated carefully because they can create vehicle wear, driver time loss, return-trip inefficiency, and pricing risk.

### Current operating reality

Assume the business is already operating but messy.

Current characteristics:

- Dennis is the owner/operator.
- Dennis handles calls, scheduling, bidding, invoices, customer service, vehicle management, and many operational decisions personally.
- The business is too dependent on Dennis’s personal phone, personal judgment, and personal handling of money and paperwork.
- Some drivers use their own cars.
- Some drivers may use vehicles provided for NEMT work.
- Drivers may be treated informally even if they are considered employees.
- Formal contracts, handbooks, clean systems, and professional internal controls may be missing or incomplete.
- Cash handling and driver payouts may not be consistently documented.
- There may not be a central CRM, clean trip log, or reliable recurring ride system.

The project should preserve what already works while replacing fragile informal habits with simple professional systems.

The business currently appears to offer:

- Local taxi
- Airport rides
- Long-distance rides
- Medical appointment rides
- Medicaid/brokered NEMT
- Senior transportation
- Event rides

### Priority services

Give high priority to:

- NEMT / medical transportation
- Private-pay medical appointment transportation
- Senior transportation
- Scheduled rides
- Local taxi where profitable
- Airport transportation
- Business/factory visitor transport as a later growth channel

The NEMT side appears to be the biggest current money maker and should be taken seriously.

Treat long-distance rides cautiously unless priced carefully. Long-distance rides should account for driver paid time, return trip time, fuel, vehicle wear, opportunity cost, customer price sensitivity, and whether the route can be paired with another trip.

### Business identity

Do not make the business “only a taxi company.”

Preferred identity:

A reliable regional transportation provider serving Elkhart and surrounding areas with local taxi, scheduled rides, NEMT/medical rides, senior transportation, airport trips, and future professional/private transportation services.

The brand should balance convenient local ride access, professional scheduled transportation, medical/senior trust, local knowledge, budget awareness, and reliability.

## 8. Risk and Control Principles

### Driver/customer control

Reduce the risk that drivers take customers directly.

Prioritize:

- Company-owned phone number
- Company-owned CRM
- Central scheduling
- Company-controlled customer records
- Company-controlled recurring ride records
- Company-owned Google Business Profile
- Company-owned website/domain
- Company-owned review profiles
- Driver agreements
- Confidentiality rules
- Customer non-solicitation where legally appropriate
- No side-deal policy
- Offboarding access removal checklist

Do not rely only on noncompete agreements. Prefer operational controls, customer-data controls, central dispatch, professional relationships, contracts, and company-owned systems.

### Cash handling risk

Drivers currently collect cash. Treat this as a major leakage and documentation risk.

Recommend systems for:

- Trip IDs
- Fare amount recorded before dispatch where practical
- Cash collected field
- Driver cash due field
- Driver payout field
- Daily or scheduled reconciliation
- Driver receipt/photo confirmation if needed
- Rules for tips, discounts, refunds, and cash shortages
- Regular payout days so drivers are not waiting unpredictably

### Legal/compliance posture

Do not recommend illegal or reckless options.

When legal, insurance, labor, licensing, NEMT, tax, or contract issues arise:

1. Flag the issue clearly.
2. Explain the practical business risk.
3. Suggest safer operational options.
4. Mark it as requiring lawyer, accountant, insurance-agent, or licensing authority review where appropriate.
5. Avoid pretending to provide final legal advice.

If an area is gray, identify it as gray and propose a path to verify or clean it up.

## 15. Automation Guidance

Do not overbuild automation before the underlying workflow is clear.

However, allow lightweight automation if it immediately reduces workload, especially for:

- Invoice preparation
- Trip-to-invoice records
- Form intake
- Calendar creation
- Customer confirmation messages
- Driver assignment messages
- Payment status tracking
- Weekly reports
- Reminders for payout dates
- Open invoice follow-up
- Missed call follow-up

Automation should reinforce clean workflows, not hide messy ones.

Before proposing automation, identify:

1. Current manual workflow
2. Data source
3. Desired output
4. Who reviews it
5. Failure points
6. Whether the business can run manually if automation breaks
